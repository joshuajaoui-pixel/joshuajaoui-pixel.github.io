🔬 Cell Biology

Animal cells contain a nucleus (controls cell), cytoplasm (where reactions happen), cell membrane (controls what enters/leaves), mitochondria (aerobic respiration) and ribosomes (protein synthesis).

Plant cells have all of the above PLUS a cell wall (support, made of cellulose), large vacuole (keeps cell rigid), and chloroplasts (photosynthesis, contain chlorophyll).

Bacterial cells are prokaryotic — no membrane-bound nucleus. They have: cell wall, cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, plasmids (small circular DNA loops), and a singular chromosomal DNA strand free in the cytoplasm.

Cell specialisation examples

  • Red blood cells — biconcave disc shape (↑ surface area), no nucleus (more room for haemoglobin), flexible
  • Nerve cells (neurones) — long axon for fast signal travel, myelin sheath insulates, many dendrites receive signals
  • Root hair cells — large surface area to absorb water & minerals, no chloroplasts (underground)
  • Sperm cells — tail for movement, many mitochondria for energy, acrosome (enzyme-filled head) to penetrate egg

Microscopy

Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size. Light microscopes: up to ×2000, can view living cells. Electron microscopes: up to ×2,000,000, much higher resolution but kill the cell.

🌿 Photosynthesis & Respiration
6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂Photosynthesis (needs light + chlorophyll). Takes place in chloroplasts.
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATPAerobic respiration. Takes place in mitochondria. Releases energy.
Glucose → Lactic acid (+ small ATP)Anaerobic respiration in animals. Builds up in muscles → cramp.
Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂Anaerobic respiration in yeast/plants (fermentation).

Limiting factors of photosynthesis

  • Light intensity — more light = faster rate (up to a point). At night, rate = 0.
  • CO₂ concentration — more CO₂ = faster rate (up to a point).
  • Temperature — too cold: enzymes slow. Too hot (above ~45°C): enzymes denature. Optimum ≈ 25–35°C.
  • Water — needed as a reactant but rarely the limiting factor in normal conditions.
🧬 Genetics & Inheritance

DNA is a double helix made of nucleotides (sugar + phosphate + base). The four bases are A, T, C, G — A pairs with T, C pairs with G (complementary base pairing). A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein.

Key terms

  • Allele — a version of a gene. Dominant (capital) always expressed; recessive (lowercase) only expressed when homozygous.
  • Homozygous — two identical alleles (AA or aa). Heterozygous — two different alleles (Aa).
  • Genotype — genetic makeup (e.g. Aa). Phenotype — physical expression (e.g. brown eyes).
  • Punnett squares — used to predict probability of offspring genotypes. e.g. Aa × Aa → 25% AA, 50% Aa, 25% aa.

Mitosis vs Meiosis

  • Mitosis: 1 division → 2 identical diploid cells. Used for growth, repair, asexual reproduction.
  • Meiosis: 2 divisions → 4 non-identical haploid cells. Used to produce gametes (sperm/eggs).
🦠 Disease & Immunity

Types of pathogen

  • Bacteria — prokaryotes. Produce toxins. Treated with antibiotics. e.g. Salmonella, TB.
  • Viruses — not cells. Hijack host cell DNA. No effective drug treatment (antivirals only). e.g. Influenza, HIV.
  • Fungi — e.g. athlete's foot, rose black spot in plants.
  • Protists — single-celled eukaryotes. e.g. Malaria (spread by mosquito vector).

Immune response

White blood cells: phagocytes engulf & destroy pathogens (phagocytosis). Lymphocytes produce antibodies specific to each antigen. Memory cells remain after infection → faster response if same pathogen returns (immunity).

Vaccines introduce a weakened/dead pathogen or antigen → immune system produces antibodies & memory cells without full disease. Antibiotics kill bacteria only — ineffective against viruses. Overuse → antibiotic resistance (MRSA).

🌍 Ecosystems & Evolution

Food chains: Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer. Energy is lost at each level (heat, movement, excretion) — only ~10% transferred.

Biodiversity: variety of species in an ecosystem. High biodiversity = more stable ecosystem. Threats: habitat destruction, pollution, introduced species, climate change.

Natural selection (Darwin): (1) Variation within population. (2) Some variants better suited to environment. (3) These individuals survive & reproduce more. (4) Advantageous traits passed on. Over many generations → evolution of new species (speciation).

⚠️ Common mistake: Evolution doesn't mean individual organisms change — populations change over generations.
🔢 Number & Algebra

Key skills — Paper 1 (no calculator)

  • Fractions: Add/subtract → common denominator. Multiply → straight across. Divide → flip the second fraction and multiply.
  • Surds: √a × √b = √(ab). Simplify √50 = √(25×2) = 5√2. Rationalise: multiply top & bottom by the surd.
  • Standard form: a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ a < 10. e.g. 0.0034 = 3.4 × 10⁻³.
  • Expanding brackets: (x+3)(x−2) = x² − 2x + 3x − 6 = x² + x − 6. Use FOIL.
  • Factorising quadratics: x² + 5x + 6 = (x+2)(x+3). Find two numbers that multiply to +6 and add to +5.
  • Sequences: nth term of arithmetic = a + (n−1)d. For geometric, multiply by common ratio each time.

Solving equations

Always do the same operation to both sides. For quadratics: factorise, complete the square, or use the formula: x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a. If b²−4ac > 0: two solutions. = 0: one solution. < 0: no real solutions.

Angles rules (must know)

  • Angles in a triangle = 180°. Angles on a straight line = 180°. Angles around a point = 360°.
  • Parallel lines: Alternate angles equal (Z-angles). Co-interior angles add to 180°. Corresponding angles equal (F-angles).
  • Polygon interior angle sum = (n−2) × 180°. Regular polygon exterior angle = 360°/n.

Volume formulas

  • Prism = cross-section area × length. Cylinder = πr²h. Cone = ⅓πr²h. Sphere = (4/3)πr³.
📊 Statistics & Probability

Averages

  • Mean = sum of values ÷ number of values. Best for symmetrical data with no outliers.
  • Median = middle value when ordered. Best when there are outliers.
  • Mode = most common value. Only average that can be non-numerical.
  • Range = max − min. Measures spread (not an average).
  • Interquartile range (IQR) = Q3 − Q1. Better measure of spread as it ignores extreme values.

Probability

P(event) = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes. Always between 0 and 1. P(not A) = 1 − P(A).

AND rule (independent events): P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B). OR rule (mutually exclusive): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).

Tree diagrams: multiply along branches (AND), add between branches (OR). Use for two-stage experiments.

⚠️ Remember: after drawing without replacement, the denominator decreases on the second branch of a tree diagram.

Ratio & proportion

  • To share in ratio a:b — find total parts, divide amount by total, multiply by each share.
  • Direct proportion: y = kx (graph is straight line through origin). Inverse proportion: y = k/x.
  • Percentage change = (change ÷ original) × 100. Percentage increase: multiply by (1 + r/100). Decrease: (1 − r/100).
  • Reverse percentage: divide by the multiplier. e.g. price after 20% increase is £120 → original = 120 ÷ 1.2 = £100.

Newton's three laws

  • 1st Law: An object stays at rest or constant velocity unless a resultant force acts on it.
  • 2nd Law: F = ma. Greater force → greater acceleration. Greater mass → smaller acceleration.
  • 3rd Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction (different objects, same type of force).

Distance-time & velocity-time graphs

  • Distance-time: gradient = speed. Flat line = stationary. Curve = changing speed.
  • Velocity-time: gradient = acceleration. Area under graph = distance travelled.

  • Resistor: limits current. Thermistor: resistance decreases as temperature increases.

⚠️ In series: if one component fails, the whole circuit stops. In parallel: other components keep working.

Electromagnetic spectrum (low freq → high freq)

Radio → Microwaves → Infrared → Visible light → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma rays. All travel at 3×10⁸ m/s in a vacuum. Higher frequency = higher energy = more dangerous.

Transverse vs Longitudinal waves

  • Transverse: oscillation perpendicular to direction of travel. e.g. light, water waves, EM waves.
  • Longitudinal: oscillation parallel to direction of travel. e.g. sound. Has compressions and rarefactions.
☢️ Radioactivity & Space

Types of nuclear radiation

  • Alpha (α): 2 protons + 2 neutrons. Stopped by paper / a few cm air. Highly ionising. Most dangerous if inhaled/ingested.
  • Beta (β): fast electron from nucleus. Stopped by a few mm aluminium. Moderately ionising.
  • Gamma (γ): electromagnetic wave from nucleus. Stopped by thick lead or concrete. Least ionising. Most penetrating.

Half-life: time for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. After 1 half-life: 50% remains. After 2: 25%. After 3: 12.5%.

The Universe

Big Bang: universe started ~13.8 billion years ago from a single point. Evidence: redshift (galaxies moving away), cosmic microwave background radiation. Life cycle of a star: nebula → protostar → main sequence → (if small: red giant → white dwarf → black dwarf. If large: red supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole).

⚛️ Atomic Structure & Periodic Table

An atom has a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in shells. Proton: +1 charge, mass 1. Neutron: 0 charge, mass 1. Electron: −1 charge, negligible mass. Atoms are neutral: protons = electrons. Atomic number = protons. Mass number = protons + neutrons. Isotopes: same element, different neutron number.

Electron configuration

Shells fill from innermost: shell 1 = max 2, shell 2 = max 8, shell 3 = max 8 (at GCSE). e.g. Sodium (11 electrons) = 2, 8, 1.

The Periodic Table

  • Group number = number of outer electrons. Period number = number of electron shells.
  • Group 1 (alkali metals): soft, low melting point, react vigorously with water → H₂ gas + metal hydroxide. Reactivity increases DOWN the group.
  • Group 7 (halogens): diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂). Reactivity DECREASES down the group. More reactive halogen displaces less reactive one.
  • Group 0 (noble gases): full outer shell → very unreactive/inert.
  • Transition metals: middle block — high density, high melting points, form coloured compounds, good catalysts.
🔗 Bonding
  • Ionic bonding: metal + non-metal. Transfer of electrons. Forms ions (+ and −). Giant ionic lattice. High melting point. Conducts when dissolved or molten.
  • Covalent bonding: non-metal + non-metal. Sharing of electron pairs. Can be simple molecular (low MP, doesn't conduct) or giant covalent (very high MP, e.g. diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide).
  • Metallic bonding: lattice of positive metal ions in a sea of delocalised electrons. High MP, good conductor of heat & electricity, malleable.
  • Graphite: layers of hexagonal carbon rings, one delocalised electron per atom → conducts electricity. Layers can slide → lubricant. Exception to "giant covalent = non-conductor".
⚗️ Reactions, Rates & Energy

Rates of reaction — four factors

  • Temperature ↑: particles have more kinetic energy → more frequent & more energetic collisions → more exceed activation energy.
  • Concentration ↑ (solutions) / Pressure ↑ (gases): more particles per volume → more frequent collisions.
  • Surface area ↑: smaller particle size → more surface exposed → more collisions.
  • Catalyst: provides alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy → more collisions succeed. Not used up.

Energy changes

  • Exothermic: releases energy. Products have lower energy than reactants. ΔH is negative. Examples: combustion, neutralisation, oxidation, respiration.
  • Endothermic: absorbs energy. Products have higher energy. ΔH is positive. Examples: thermal decomposition, photosynthesis, citric acid + sodium hydrogen carbonate.

Acids & bases

Acid + base → salt + water (neutralisation). Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen. Acid + metal carbonate → salt + water + CO₂. pH scale 0−14: 0−6 acid, 7 neutral, 8−14 alkaline.

🛢️ Organic Chemistry & Electrolysis

Hydrocarbons

  • Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Saturated (only single C−C bonds). e.g. methane CH₄, ethane C₂H₆. Used as fuels.
  • Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ. Unsaturated (C=C double bond). e.g. ethene C₂H₄. React with bromine water → decolourises (addition reaction). Alkanes do NOT decolourise bromine water.
  • Combustion: complete = CO₂ + H₂O. Incomplete (not enough O₂) = CO (toxic) + H₂O + soot (carbon).
  • Cracking: breaking long-chain alkanes into shorter, more useful alkanes + alkenes. Thermal cracking (high temp/pressure) or catalytic cracking (zeolite catalyst, lower temp).

Electrolysis

Uses electricity to break down ionic compounds when molten or dissolved. Cathode (−): positive ions (cations) gain electrons → reduced. Anode (+): negative ions (anions) lose electrons → oxidised. Electrolysis of water: H₂ at cathode, O₂ at anode. Electrolysis of brine (NaCl): Cl₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode, NaOH in solution.

⚠️ OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).
